Demo Review & Coaching Guide

Demo coaching usually stops somewhere between 30 and 90 days, right about the time the SE has mastered the "show-up and throw-up" demo: the one that jams all the features and benefits into a 50-minute monologue with five minutes at the end dedicated to "any questions?" This gives you a consistent way to review one, score it, and turn that score into a coaching conversation that actually changes the next demo.

Most demo feedback sounds like "good job" or "that felt long." Neither one tells an SE what to do differently next time. This guide replaces the gut-feel version with a rubric, a review process, and a coaching conversation built to change behavior, not just record an opinion.

What's inside

Seven sections, from the thinking behind the rubric to a scorecard you fill out as you go.

  • The philosophy: three principles to keep in view while you watch and score, including why a demo should play like a Lego set built for the room in front of you, not a scripted monologue
  • The scoring rubric: seven dimensions, from discovery and framing to call control, each scored 1 to 5 with a plain description of what weak and strong actually look like
  • How to run the review: watch with the rubric open, timestamp what jumps out, and land on one strength and one focus before you ever open your mouth
  • The coaching conversation: how to lead with what worked, balance the positives against the criticism, and end every session with a specific next rep instead of just a score
  • An SE self-audit checklist: a shorter version SEs run on their own recordings between coaching sessions, so review time goes to the harder stuff
  • Common failure patterns and fixes: the patterns that show up again and again, each with a one-line fix
  • A fillable scorecard: the whole rubric in an appendix you fill out and save per demo, or print

How to use it

Watch the recording once with the rubric open and score as you go. Then sit down with your SE and lead with what worked before you get to the one thing you'd change. The rubric gives you the score. The conversation is what actually makes someone better.
